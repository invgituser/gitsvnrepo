What does it mean to be a Registered Investment Adviser?

A Registered Investment Adviser is an Investment Adviser registered with the Securities and Exchange Commission or a state’s securities agency. Individuals or firms that receive compensation for giving advice on investing in securities such as stocks, bonds, mutual funds, or exchange traded funds are generally deemed to be investment advisers.

What is meant by a Fiduciary Standard?

This means that we are required to act solely in the best interest of our clients, even if that interest is in conflict with our own financial interest. RIAs must disclose any conflict of interest, or any potential conflict of interest, to our clients before and throughout a business engagement.

What is an ETF?

In the simplest terms, Exchange-Traded Funds (ETFs) are investment products that track indexes like the S&P 500, Dow Jones or NASDAQ-100 Index at a minimal cost. When you buy shares of an ETF, you are buying shares of a portfolio that tracks the yield and return of its underlying index. The main difference between ETFs and other types of index funds is that ETFs don’t try to outperform their corresponding index, but simply replicate its performance.

There are many advantages to including ETFs in your investment portfolio:

    • ETFs act like indexes and follow certain market sectors. However, unlike an index, you can purchase an ETF with one single transaction. You are purchasing a mini-portfolio.
    • Capital gains taxes are generally lower for ETFs than traditional mutual funds due to the structure of each trade. When a gain is realized in a daily mutual fund trade or even within an index trade, capital gain taxes are incurred immediately. In the case of exchange traded funds, the individual capital gains are not realized until the assets are sold with the entire fund. Therefore ETFs are considered a “”tax friendly” investment.
    • ETFs trade throughout market hours. ETFs prices are continuously updated during the trading day. In other words ETFs trade just like equities on the stock market.

How do you determine my Risk Tolerance?

We construct a client’s portfolio by selecting an appropriate allocation of investments among four core asset classes: stocks, bonds, commodities and cash. We use money market Treasury bills as the risk-free cash asset and gold as the commodity.

Each asset class has a different degree of risk. The riskiest assets, such as stocks, tend to have the highest long-term returns, whereas lower-risk short-term bonds or Treasury bills tend to have much lower returns. Likewise, there is a higher probability of significant negative returns with higher-risk assets, such as stocks, as compared with short-term fixed income securities. We use very long-term historical returns to assign risk to each asset type.

When making an asset allocation recommendation, we translate your profile to an appropriate level of risk. We then create a mix of asset classes, from lowest to highest risk, to construct an optimal combination of assets for you. This asset allocation diversification helps reduce risk. Invessence preserves diversification by regular rebalancing.

We believe that investing should have long-term objectives. Past returns are never a guarantee for future returns but are simply used as a guide for goal calculations. Unlike some advisers who make predictions about future returns with very long disclaimers, we know we cannot predict the future. However, we also know there is a consistency in market returns over long periods of time. Therefore, individuals with long-term goals can invest in riskier assets and those with shorter-term goals may do better to invest in less risky assets, such as certificates of deposit, Treasury bills, and municipal bonds.
